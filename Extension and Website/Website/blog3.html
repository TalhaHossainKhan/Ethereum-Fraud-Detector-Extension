What is stopping Bangladesh from
implementing cryptocurrency as a
legal tender

Published on June 30, 2023

The journey toward crypto currency’s widespread adoption as a legal tender is marred by a multitude of challenges, especially in developing countries like Bangladesh.

One of the primary concerns that deter Bangladesh from embracing cryptocurrencies is the lack of a regulatory framework. The decentralized nature of cryptocurrencies poses significant challenges for regulatory bodies. As of 2021, the absence of robust regulations exposes the market to potential fraud, money laundering, and tax evasion, making the Bangladesh government wary of their implementation.

Volatility stands as another major obstacle on the path to cryptocurrency integration. The cryptocurrency market is notorious for its price fluctuations. For instance, Bitcoin, the pioneering cryptocurrency, experienced a staggering price surge from around $1,000 in January 2017 to nearly $20,000 in December 2017, only to plummet to approximately $3,000 in December 2018. Such volatility undermines the stability required for a currency to function effectively as a legal tender, creating apprehension among policymakers.

Furthermore, ensuring consumer protection remains a paramount concern. Cryptocurrencies, with their intricate technological foundations, often baffle the average consumer. Lack of understanding and education exposes individuals to scams and investment losses, raising ethical concerns for the Bangladesh government.

From a macroeconomic perspective, the introduction of cryptocurrencies challenges a nation’s ability to exercise effective monetary policies. Central banks rely on various tools to control the money supply and stabilize economies. The advent of decentralized currencies threatens to disrupt these established mechanisms, raising questions about the efficacy of monetary policy control in the face of this digital revolution.

Technological challenges also loom large in the debate surrounding cryptocurrency adoption. Implementing cryptocurrencies on a national scale necessitates a robust technological infrastructure. Developing countries like Bangladesh may lack the necessary resources and expertise, hindering their ability to integrate cryptocurrencies seamlessly into their existing financial systems.

Additionally, the global acceptance of cryptocurrencies remains a significant hurdle. Despite their growing popularity, cryptocurrencies are far from achieving universal acceptance. Businesses and countries around the world vary in their readiness to embrace digital currencies, creating barriers for international trade and financial transactions if cryptocurrencies were to be adopted as legal tender.

Lastly, international pressure and influence cannot be overlooked. Powerful nations and international organizations exert significant sway over the policies of developing countries. In the context of cryptocurrencies, external pressure against their adoption could stifle a nation’s ability to explore this opportunity fully.
